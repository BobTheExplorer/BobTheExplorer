When the thought of abandoned or, dare I say, haunted psychiatric centers comes to mind, most locals think of the former Kings Park Psychiatric Center(KPPC). Even those who do not consider themselves all too adventurous can be seen on the grounds, along with the elderly and young. It is not an uncommon sight to run into another person when exploring any of the buildings on the 368-acre-long campus. But the person you encounter is not always another keen explorer or juvenile graffiti artist. As cops have been known to frequently patrol within buildings № #93 & № #7, handing out tickets to whoever is unlucky enough to encounter them.

On August 25, 1988, the state-run Office of Mental Health would declare an overcrowding crisis within hospitals and psychiatric centers alike. Patients are laid on beds that are hardly even inches from one another, to them, it is degrading and to the staff a logistical nightmare. Due to the overflowing number of patients being received, many are carted off to other state-run facilities for treatment.

Many would see the monolithic 159-foot structure known as building No. 93 as they undergo the process of institutionalization, afterwards they would find residence on the active floors. Despite having 13 floors, only 1/3 of them were in use; the remaining 2/3 had been closed off since the 1970s. In 1996, the entirety of the building, along with many others on the grounds, would lock their doors permanently. Some buildings would still operate afterward; building No. 7 would receive electricity until 2001, and building No. 1 had served as administrative office until 2012.

Post-abandonment the campus became the subject of heated controversy over it's future. The Kings Park question is a dividing one, what should be done with such a large derelict facility?

Historians advocate for the preservation of the buildings on site that have yet to be demolished, based mainly on the historical significance that some of the buildings hold. Many were constructed during the turn of the 20th century, yet this among many other facts is are ignored by the state of New York State Office of Parks, Recreation, and Historic Preservation. The state, which runs the park had revitalized the waterfront with their Local Waterfront Revitalization Program, yet they razed 16 buildings and structures. Glancing over the whole "protect, restore, and rehabilitate locally significant historic sites in the Sunken Meadow State Park and the Kings Park Psychiatric Center."

Others view the buildings as a blight upon the Smithtown community, polluting the park that it occupies. Razing them would open a new chapter of history within the town. Many could be hired to partake in the razing of the buildings, and new commercial buildings could take their space. The area could be developed into the next community center, one in which electroshock therapy and lobotomies are relics of the past
