In 1907, all four existing state mental institutions in New York were located upstate, more specifically in the Syracuse, Newark, Rome, and Sonyea regions. As a result of this, the State Board of Charities was strongly considering building a fifth somewhere in the downstate region.


At the time, the "feeble-minded" were viewed as a moral menace, meaning they were blamed for crimes like assault, murder, arson, or even sexual perversion. Organizations like the State Charities Aid Association viewed segregation and institutionalization as the only solution.


Modeled after the already-existing Craig Colony for Epileptics, the idea of a Eastern New York Custodial Asylum was proposed by the committee in their 1908 report. Soon after this, the committee changed the name to Letchworth village in honor of fellow board member William Pryor Letchworth.


Retiring at the age of 46 due to success in the steel and saddle industry, William Letchworth devoted his fortune and the rest of his life to aiding the poor and unwell. He joins the State Board of Charities and campaigns the state to remove all children from poorhouses, orphan aslyums, and juvenile reformatories.


When the plans for the village were first drafted in 1910, $457,000 was approved by New York state legislature for the construction of ten buildings: an attendants' home, dining hall, power plant, storehouse, laundry building, and four dormitories. Funds for a water supply and sewage disposal system, as well as minor alterations to the existing buildings to increase their capacity, were secured that same year.


Famous landscape architect Frederick Law Olmstead, who had also designed Prospect Park, was employed to design the 2,300 acre grounds of Letchworth Village. Much of the land was used to grow crops and that were tended to by the patients until the 1960s.


Up to 400 acres of the property was used for the purpose of cultivation and care of livestock; yielding 300 tons of hay, 600 tons of corn, and 2,000 bushels of corn to feed the farm animals. Autarky or the act of self-sufficiency was a key focus of many of the earlier farm colony styled state schools.


Several judges and lawyers from New York City petitioned the then governor of New York on March 30th, 1917 for additional accommodations for the "feeble-minded". Comparing the situation of building eight new dormitories at the village to the new cottage constructed at the Newark State Custodial Asylum for disorderly feeble-minded women.


Since there were no state schools inside of the city, all of the "mentally defective delinquents" and "feeble-minded" were sent to Letchworth and later on Rockland Psychiatric Center. With its location being just north of the city, close to 90% of the patient population were from the city, while the other 10% were made up those from other counties like Nassau or Orange county.


The term "feeble-minded" was mainly used by eugenicists who saw this condition of the mind as the root cause behind crime, alcoholism, and other societal issues. Charles Davenport, George Washington Corner, and T.H. Painter chemically castrated a "mongoloid dwarf" at Letchworth in 1929.


Trisomy 21, a condition directly linked to mental retardation and physical deformities, was once designated as "Mongolism" by biologists. Davenport aimed to study the condition by doing a cytological examination of the testis belonging to a 13-year-old boy admitted to Letchworth; his father was dead, and his mother was of "low mentality," which made gaining her consent for the procedure easy for the three men.


Scientific research had once been a focal point of the institute but was severely hampered due to the lack of funding from Albany and the start of US involvement in World War II. In September 1970, staffing changes in the administration shifted the goals of the facility from providing custodial care to rehabilitating patients so that they may be able to reintegrate into society.


Custodial care was taking care of severely disabled patients who were unable to look after themselves without the constant assistance of an aide. Staff shortages and the overadmission of patients made this type of care nearly impossible to do, resulting in cases of malnourishment caused by neglect.


As part of this shift in direction, efforts to combat severe overcrowding were attempted, mainly by preventing the number of admissions labeled as unnecessary and returning rehabilitated patients back to their communities.


A year before his death in 1942, the photographer Arnold Genthe who was best known for his coverage of the 1906 San Francisco earthquake, captured the terrible conditions present inside of the institution. Around the same time, the Temporary Commission on State Hospital Problems created by Governor Herbert Lehman was focused on cutting costs rather than addressing the persistent issue of overcrowding.


There were 3,908 admitted patients, which was 730% over the normal capacity of this institution in 1942. Hospitalizations and malnutrition became a critical issue with the younger patient population; many of them required individual care from nurses to be properly fed.

Overcrowding continued well into 1948, when the institution had 4,460 admitted patients when the capacity limit was set at 3,178. With no room for extra beds, 500 children slept on mattresses laid out all across the floor of the dorms and the dayrooms.


Thomas E. Dewey, the 47th Governor of New York, declared on October 29th, 1950, that "not one child is sleeping on a floor bed at Letchworth Village." Four new dorm buildings containing 480 beds were constructed that same year. Additional funds to the Department of Mental Hygiene were provided, upping the annual budget for that year to $116,000,000 when it was just $41,000,000 in 1942.
